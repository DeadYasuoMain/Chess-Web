Chess, as we know it today, has a history spanning over 1500 years, originating in India during the 6th century with a game called Chaturanga. This game then spread to Persia, where it evolved into Shatranj, and eventually, through various cultural exchanges, made its way to the Muslim world and then to Europe. The modern rules of chess, including the moves of the pieces, were largely finalized in Europe by the 15th century.
Here's a more detailed look at the timeline:

  • 6th Century, India:

    The game of Chaturanga is believed to have originated in India during the Gupta Empire. Chaturanga involved different pieces with distinct powers, and victory was determined by checkmating the opponent's king.

  • 7th Century, Persia:

    Chaturanga spread to Persia and was adapted into Shatranj, which included some rule changes.

  • 7th Century onwards, Muslim World:

    The game continued to spread through the Muslim world after the Islamic conquest of Persia.

  • 10th-11th Centuries, Europe:

    Through trade and cultural exchange, Shatranj reached Southern Europe, particularly Italy and Spain.

  • 15th Century, Europe:

    The modern rules of chess, with significant changes to piece movement, particularly the queen and bishop, began to emerge in Europe.

  • 19th Century:

    Formalized chess tournaments, the use of chess clocks, and the establishment of the World Chess Federation (FIDE) marked the beginning of modern chess organization and competition.

  • 20th Century & Beyond:

    The game saw further theoretical development, the rise of powerful chess engines, and the continued growth of chess as a global pastime.
